Week 1 — Environment & Philosophy

Learning Objectives

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Explain why reproducibility matters in clinical research
  • Distinguish between reproducibility, repeatability, and replicability
  • Identify common methodological failures that lead to retraction
  • Use Retraction Watch to find case studies of failed research
  • Recognize the hallmarks of irreproducible anesthesia research

Why Reproducibility Matters

Anesthesia practice depends on evidence. When we choose an induction dose, a monitoring technique, or a rescue protocol, we assume the evidence base is sound. But evidence fails when research cannot be reproduced.

Clinical stakes: Irreproducible findings enter guidelines. Incorrect guidelines harm patients. Retractions damage trust in our specialty.

Your role: As a clinician who reads and contributes to the literature, you are a gatekeeper. Learn to recognize weak evidence before it shapes your practice.

Core Concepts

Definitions

Term Meaning Example
Repeatability Same team, same setup, same result Lab reruns the analysis; gets identical coefficients
Reproducibility Different team, same data, same result Independent analyst downloads your dataset; recreates your figures
Replicability Different team, different data, similar result New study in different hospital confirms your finding

We focus on reproducibility in this tier. It is the foundation — without it, replicability is meaningless.

The Reproducibility Crisis

Estimates suggest 50–90% of published research findings cannot be reproduced. Causes include:

  • Unavailable data or code — the “trust me” approach
  • P-hacking — running multiple analyses, reporting only significant ones
  • HARKing — Hypothesizing After Results are Known
  • Statistical errors — incorrect tests, multiple comparison failures
  • Fraud — fabrication, falsification (rare but catastrophic)

Retraction Watch

Retraction Watch (retractionwatch.com) maintains a database of retracted papers with reasons. It is a teaching tool: every retraction contains a lesson.

Common reasons in anesthesia literature:

Reason Example Pattern
Data fabrication Patient records do not match hospital database
Statistical error Incorrect analysis chosen; p-values invalid
Unavailable data Authors decline to share raw data upon request
Ethical violation Lack of IRB approval for human subjects
Image manipulation Western blot bands duplicated or altered

Exercise

Part 1: Explore Retraction Watch

  1. Go to retractionwatch.com
  2. Search for “anesthesia” or “anesthesiology”
  3. Select one retracted paper from a mainstream journal (Anesthesia & Analgesia, British Journal of Anaesthesia, Anesthesiology)
  4. Read the retraction notice (usually linked)
  5. Note: year of publication, year of retraction, stated reason

Part 2: Identify the Failure

For your selected paper, answer:

  • What was the core claim? (What did the authors say they found?)
  • What methodological failure led to retraction?
  • Was the error detectable from the published paper alone?
  • Could sharing raw data or code have prevented this?

Part 3: Reflect

Write 3–5 sentences:

What warning signs might a critical reader have noticed before the retraction? What does this teach us about our responsibility as consumers of anesthesia research?

Save your response as week-01-reflection.md in your course repository.

Common Pitfalls in Anesthesia Research

Pitfall Why It Happens How to Spot It
Underpowered study Small N, rare outcomes Confidence intervals include null; post-hoc power calculations
Multiple comparisons unchecked Many secondary outcomes No Bonferroni, FDR, or other adjustment; p-values cluster near 0.05
Selective reporting Negative primary outcome Protocol pre-specified outcomes differ from published outcomes
Confounding by indication Retrospective drug comparison Groups differ systematically (sicker patients get drug X)
Missing data mishandling Dropouts, incomplete records No description of missingness; complete-case analysis only
